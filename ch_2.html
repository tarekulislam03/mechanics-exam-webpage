Core concepts

Centre of Gravity (CG)
The point where the entire weight of a body is assumed to act. It is a physical point that depends on the gravitational field. For uniform gravity, CG = centroid.
Centroid
A purely geometric property — the mean position of all points of a figure. For areas, ̄x = ΣAx / ΣA and ȳ = ΣAy / ΣA. Does NOT depend on gravity.
CG vs Centroid
CG = centroid only when: (1) body is homogeneous (uniform density), and (2) gravitational field is uniform. Otherwise they differ.
First Moment of Area
Q = ∫y dA about X-axis; Q = ∫x dA about Y-axis. Used to find centroid. Units: m³ or mm³.
Area Moment of Inertia (Second Moment of Area)
I = ∫y² dA. Measures resistance to bending/rotation. Higher I = stiffer beam. Units: m⁴ or mm⁴. NOT the same as mass MI.
Parallel Axis Theorem
I_AB = I_G + A·d². The MI about any axis = MI about parallel centroidal axis + Area × (distance between axes)². d is measured from centroid to the new axis.
Perpendicular Axis Theorem
For a lamina in XY plane: I_z = I_x + I_y. The polar MI = sum of MIs about two perpendicular axes in the plane. Applies ONLY to 2D planar figures.
Radius of Gyration (k)
k = √(I/A). The distance from the axis at which the entire area could be concentrated to give the same MI. Used in column buckling (slenderness ratio = L/k).
Composite Section Method
Break complex shape into simple shapes (rectangles, triangles, circles). Treat holes/cutouts as negative areas. Find centroid then use PAT for total MI.
Axis of Symmetry
The centroid always lies on any axis of symmetry. For doubly-symmetric sections (rectangle, circle, I-section), centroid is at the geometric center.

Centroid formulas

Centroid of composite area (x̄, ȳ)
x̄ = (ΣAᵢxᵢ) / ΣAᵢ    ȳ = (ΣAᵢyᵢ) / ΣAᵢ
Centroid from first principles
x̄ = (1/A) ∫x dA    ȳ = (1/A) ∫y dA
Centroid of triangle (from base)
ȳ = h/3  (from base)    ȳ = 2h/3  (from apex)
Centroid of semicircle (from diameter)
ȳ = 4R / 3π ≈ 0.424R
Centroid of quarter circle
x̄ = ȳ = 4R / 3π

Moment of inertia formulas

From first principles
I_xx = ∫y² dA    I_yy = ∫x² dA
Parallel Axis Theorem
I_AB = I_G + A · d²
Perpendicular Axis Theorem (planar bodies only)
I_z = I_x + I_y   (Polar MI = J = I_xx + I_yy)
Radius of gyration
k = √(I / A)    or    I = A · k²

Steps for composite MI

1. Divide into simple shapes (label cutouts negative)
2. Find centroid of each shape (x̄ᵢ, ȳᵢ) from reference axis
3. Find ȳ of composite = ΣAᵢȳᵢ / ΣAᵢ
4. Find I_G of each shape about its own centroidal axis
5. Apply PAT: I_total = Σ(I_Gᵢ + Aᵢ·dᵢ²) where dᵢ = ȳᵢ − ȳ
Note: In PAT, d is always from the CENTROID of each sub-shape to the CENTROIDAL AXIS of the composite — not from the base line. This is the most common exam mistake.

Solved example problems

Q1. Find the centroid of a T-section: Flange 100mm × 20mm on top, Web 20mm × 80mm below.
Reference axis: bottom of web (bottom of section). Total height = 100 mm.
Flange: A₁ = 100×20 = 2000 mm², ȳ₁ = 80 + 10 = 90 mm
Web: A₂ = 20×80 = 1600 mm², ȳ₂ = 80/2 = 40 mm
ΣA = 2000 + 1600 = 3600 mm²
ȳ = (2000×90 + 1600×40) / 3600 = (180000 + 64000) / 3600 = 244000/3600
ȳ = 67.78 mm from bottom
Q2. Find MI of a rectangle 60mm wide × 90mm deep about centroidal X-X axis from first principles.
Take a strip of width b=60mm and thickness dy at distance y from centroid.
dA = b·dy = 60 dy
I_xx = ∫y²·dA from −d/2 to +d/2 = ∫₋₄₅⁺⁴⁵ y²·60 dy
= 60 · [y³/3]₋₄₅⁺⁴⁵ = 60 × (2×45³/3) = 60 × (2×91125/3)
= 60 × 60750 = 3,645,000 mm⁴ = 3.645×10⁶ mm⁴
Check using formula: bd³/12 = 60×90³/12 = 60×729000/12 = 3,645,000 mm⁴ ✓
Q3. Find MI of the T-section from Q1 about its centroidal X-X axis. (ȳ = 67.78 mm from bottom)
For Flange (100×20): I_G1 = 100×20³/12 = 66,667 mm⁴; d₁ = 90−67.78 = 22.22 mm
I₁ = 66,667 + 2000×22.22² = 66,667 + 987,765 = 1,054,432 mm⁴
For Web (20×80): I_G2 = 20×80³/12 = 853,333 mm⁴; d₂ = 67.78−40 = 27.78 mm
I₂ = 853,333 + 1600×27.78² = 853,333 + 1,234,028 = 2,087,361 mm⁴
I_total = 1,054,432 + 2,087,361 = 3,141,793 mm⁴ ≈ 3.14×10⁶ mm⁴
Q4. Find the centroid of a semicircle of radius 50 mm from its diameter using first principles.
Consider a horizontal strip at height y, width = 2√(R²−y²), thickness dy
dA = 2√(R²−y²) dy
ȳ = (1/A)∫₀ᴿ y·2√(R²−y²) dy where A = πR²/2
Let u = R²−y², du = −2y dy → ∫y·√(R²−y²) dy = −(1/3)(R²−y²)^(3/2)
∫₀ᴿ = [−(1/3)(R²−y²)^(3/2)]₀ᴿ = 0−(−R³/3) = R³/3
ȳ = (2×R³/3) / (πR²/2) = (2R³/3)×(2/πR²) = 4R/3π
For R=50: ȳ = 4×50/(3π) = 200/9.424 = 21.22 mm from diameter
Q5. An I-section has: Top flange 80×10, Web 10×100, Bottom flange 120×10 (all in mm). Find centroid and I_xx about centroidal axis.
Reference: bottom. Total height = 120 mm.
Bottom flange: A₁=120×10=1200, ȳ₁=5 mm
Web: A₂=10×100=1000, ȳ₂=10+50=60 mm
Top flange: A₃=80×10=800, ȳ₃=110+5=115 mm
ΣA=3000 mm²; ȳ=(1200×5+1000×60+800×115)/3000=(6000+60000+92000)/3000=158000/3000
ȳ = 52.67 mm from bottom
I₁=120×10³/12+1200×(52.67−5)²=10000+1200×2272=2,736,400 mm⁴
I₂=10×100³/12+1000×(60−52.67)²=833333+1000×53.7=886,653 mm⁴
I₃=80×10³/12+800×(115−52.67)²=6667+800×3889=3,117,867 mm⁴
I_total = 2,736,400+886,653+3,117,867 = 6,740,920 mm⁴ ≈ 6.74×10⁶ mm⁴

Mass moment of inertia — key concepts

Definition
I = ∫r² dm. Resistance of a body to angular acceleration. Analogous to mass in linear dynamics. Units: kg·m².
vs Area MI
Area MI = ∫y² dA (geometry only, units mm⁴ or m⁴). Mass MI = ∫r² dm (involves mass distribution, units kg·m²). Area MI is used in bending theory; Mass MI is used in rotational dynamics.
Relationship
For a uniform lamina of density ρ and thickness t: I_mass = ρ·t · I_area. So Mass MI = density × thickness × Area MI.
Parallel Axis Theorem (mass)
I = I_G + M·d². Same form as area PAT but M = total mass instead of area A.
Radius of Gyration (k)
k = √(I/M). Distance from axis where all mass could be concentrated to give same rotational inertia. Used in dynamics and vibration analysis.
Physical significance
Torque τ = I·α (analogous to F = ma). A flywheel stores energy = ½Iω². Higher I = harder to start/stop rotation. Key in engine design, gears, rotating machinery.
